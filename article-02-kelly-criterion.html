How Much Should You Bet?

Most investors obsess over what to buy. The Kelly Criterion answers a more important question: how much of your capital should you actually put in.

R
Rohan Tainwala
CFA L1 · MFin · MoM · BBA · NISM XIX-C

In 1956, a mathematician at Bell Labs named John Kelly published a paper on information theory. He was not writing about investing. He was solving a problem about signal transmission over noisy telephone lines. And yet, the formula he derived went on to become one of the most quietly influential ideas in the history of money management. Hedge funds use it. Professional poker players swear by it. And most retail investors have never heard of it.

It is called the Kelly Criterion. And it answers a question that most people never think to ask: not what to invest in, but exactly how much of your capital to put behind any given bet.

Get this wrong in one direction and you are under-investing, leaving returns on the table. Get it wrong in the other direction and a single bad run can wipe you out. The Kelly Criterion finds the precise point in between.

Risk comes from not knowing what you are doing.

Warren Buffett

The Problem Kelly Was Solving

Before we get to the formula, it helps to understand the problem. Imagine you have an edge. Not a huge one, but a real one. You win 55% of the time on a bet that pays 1:1. Over time, you should make money. That much is obvious.

But here is what is not obvious: how much you bet on each round matters enormously. Bet too little and your edge compounds slowly. Bet too much and a string of losses, which will come eventually, can destroy your capital before the edge has time to work in your favour. Even if you have a genuine advantage, reckless sizing can ruin you.

Kelly's insight was that there is a mathematically optimal fraction of your capital to wager on each bet, given your win probability and the payoff ratio. Bet that fraction consistently and you maximise the long-run growth rate of your wealth. Bet more than that fraction and your growth rate actually falls. Bet twice the Kelly amount and you are, on average, going nowhere at all.

Position sizing is not a secondary concern. It is the difference between a good strategy working and a good strategy destroying you.

The Formula

The Kelly Criterion is expressed as a fraction of your total capital to deploy on a single opportunity. Here it is:

The Kelly Criterion
f* = p − (1 − p) / b
f* Fraction of capital to invest p Probability of winning 1−p Probability of losing b Net odds received on the bet

The variable b is the net profit per unit risked. If you invest ₹100 and a win returns ₹150, your net gain is ₹50, so b = 0.5. If you invest ₹100 and a win returns ₹200, your net gain is ₹100, so b = 1. Think of it as the reward-to-risk ratio of the opportunity.

Let us work through a clean example before things get more interesting.

Example 1 The Coin Flip With an Edge

You are offered a coin flip. Heads you win 2x your stake, tails you lose your stake. Someone has verified this coin lands heads 60% of the time. You have ₹1,00,000 to deploy. How much should you bet?

Your inputs: p = 0.60, b = 2 (you win double your stake on a win), so (1 - p) = 0.40

f* = 0.60 − (0.40 / 2)
f* = 0.60 − 0.20
f* = 0.40, or 40% of your capital

Kelly says bet ₹40,000. Not ₹10,000 because you feel cautious. Not ₹80,000 because you feel confident. Exactly ₹40,000, because that is the fraction that maximises the long-run growth of your wealth given this specific edge.

Now Apply It to Investing

Coins are a clean teaching tool. But investing is messier. You rarely know your win probability with precision. You are dealing with partial positions, transaction costs, and opportunities that do not reset cleanly after each round. So how does Kelly translate?

The honest answer is: it translates as a framework for thinking, not as an exact prescription. The inputs are never perfectly known in markets. But the discipline the formula enforces is enormously valuable even when the numbers are approximate.

Example 2 A Stock Position

You have done your analysis on a stock. Based on your research, you believe there is a 65% probability it rises 40% over the next year. You also believe there is a 35% chance it falls 20%.

Your inputs: p = 0.65, b = 0.40 / 0.20 = 2 (reward-to-risk ratio), (1 - p) = 0.35

f* = 0.65 − (0.35 / 2)
f* = 0.65 − 0.175
f* = 0.475, or approximately 47% of your capital

This is the Full Kelly. In practice, most professional investors use Half Kelly or even Quarter Kelly, which we will come to shortly. But this number tells you something important: given your stated edge on this trade, concentrating nearly half your portfolio here is mathematically defensible. Most people would never go that high. The formula asks why not.

Example 3 When There Is No Edge

You are looking at a trade where your honest assessment gives you a 50% win probability and a 1:1 payoff ratio. Equal chance of winning or losing the same amount.

f* = 0.50 − (0.50 / 1)
f* = 0.50 − 0.50
f* = 0.00, or invest nothing

Kelly returns zero. The formula is telling you clearly: there is no edge here, so the optimal bet is to not play. This is perhaps the most useful output Kelly produces. It forces you to confront whether you actually have an edge before you deploy capital.

Full Kelly, Half Kelly, and Why Professionals Go Smaller

The Full Kelly fraction maximises long-run geometric growth. Theoretically it is optimal. In practice, very few professionals use it without modification. Here is why.

The Kelly formula assumes you know your win probability precisely. You never do. If you overestimate your edge even slightly, and most people do, Full Kelly leads to over-betting. And over-betting at Kelly is not just suboptimal. The mathematics are surprisingly punishing about it.

Bet 2x Kelly and your long-run growth rate drops to zero. Bet 3x Kelly and you are actively destroying wealth in expectation. The curve between Kelly and ruin is steep and asymmetric.

Strategy Capital Deployed Growth Rate Drawdown Risk
Quarter Kelly 25% of f* Moderate Very Low
Half Kelly 50% of f* 75% of max growth Low
Full Kelly 100% of f* Maximum theoretical Significant volatility
2x Kelly 200% of f* Zero or negative Very High

Half Kelly is a particularly elegant compromise. You give up only 25% of the maximum possible growth rate, but you dramatically reduce the volatility of your equity curve. For most investors who are honest about the uncertainty in their probability estimates, Half Kelly is the more rational choice.

The Kelly fraction is a ceiling, not a target. In real markets, with uncertain inputs, starting below it is almost always the wiser choice.

The Pitfalls You Need to Know

Kelly is a powerful tool. It is also easy to misuse. There are a few failure modes worth understanding before you apply it.

Overconfidence in your probability estimates. The formula is only as good as its inputs. If you think you win 65% of your trades but your actual win rate over a large sample is 52%, the Kelly fraction you calculate will be far too large. The formula cannot save you from bad self-assessment. Be conservative with your probability estimates, especially early on.

Applying Kelly to correlated positions. The formula assumes independence between bets. In a portfolio context, if your positions are correlated, which most equity positions are during market stress, the effective Kelly fraction for the portfolio is lower than the sum of the individual Kelly fractions. Running multi-asset Kelly requires adjusting for correlation, which most simplified implementations ignore.

Ignoring liquidity and time horizon. Kelly optimises for long-run growth. If you need capital back within a year, the short-run volatility of a Full Kelly strategy can be brutal. This connects directly back to the golden rule from our first article: never invest money you might need in the short term.

Important Limitation

Kelly assumes you can estimate win probability accurately and that outcomes are independent across bets. In real markets, neither condition holds perfectly. Use Kelly as a framework for disciplined sizing, not as a precision instrument. When uncertain, size down, not up.

What Kelly Actually Teaches You

Beyond the arithmetic, the Kelly Criterion forces a discipline that most investors lack. Before you deploy capital on any trade or investment, it demands that you answer three specific questions. What is the probability I am right? What do I win if I am right? What do I lose if I am wrong?

Most people skip all three. They buy because a stock looks interesting, or because someone they respect mentioned it, or because it has gone up recently and feels like it will keep going. Kelly has no patience for any of that. It requires you to quantify your edge, or admit you do not have one.

That process alone, separate from any calculation, is worth the effort. Sitting down and honestly estimating your probability of being right on a trade is a humbling exercise for most people. It should be. Most trades have thinner edges than their owners believe.

The investors who internalise Kelly tend to do two things differently as a result. They take larger positions on their highest-conviction ideas than they would have otherwise. And they walk away from low-edge opportunities they might previously have taken out of boredom or FOMO. Both of those adjustments, taken together, tend to meaningfully improve long-run outcomes.

A Practical Starting Point

If you want to start using Kelly in your own investing, here is a sensible approach. Start by using it diagnostically rather than prescriptively. For your next ten investment ideas, before you size the position, run the Kelly calculation. Use conservative probability estimates. See what the formula tells you relative to what you were planning to do.

You will likely find one of two things. Either you were significantly under-sizing your best ideas, holding back on your highest-conviction positions out of a general instinct for caution. Or you were over-sizing mediocre opportunities where, when forced to state your win probability explicitly, your edge is thinner than you thought.

Both discoveries are valuable. Kelly does not guarantee good returns. No formula does. What it provides is a rational framework for translating conviction into capital allocation, one that has stood up to scrutiny across decades of professional use in markets, sports betting, and beyond.

Most people focus relentlessly on finding better opportunities. Kelly reminds you that knowing how much to bet on the opportunities you already have is just as important. Often more so.
